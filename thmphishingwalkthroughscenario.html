I ran this scenario inside TryHackMe's SOC Simulator — a training environment built to feel like a real alert queue instead of a quiz. I picked Splunk as my SIEM, started the clock, and watched five alerts land back to back. Case files

Five alerts, in the order they arrived. Each one closed with a verdict and a reason.

#8814 Email 11/20 · 20:02:00

"Action Required: Finalize Your Onboarding Profile"

Fromonboarding@hrconnex.thm
Toj.garcia@thetrydaily.thm
Linkhrconnex.thm/onboarding/15400654060/j.garcia
third-party vendor confirmed no urgency language domain not spoofed

First instinct: flag it — an unsolicited onboarding link is exactly the shape of a phishing email. But pivoting on hrconnex.thm in the SIEM turned up an internal message from HR describing it as a brand-new third-party onboarding partner, sent because Garcia's profile link genuinely hadn't arrived yet. No spoofed sender, no pressure tactics, and now a legitimate business reason for the email to exist at all.

False Positive

Fromurgents@amazon.biz
Toh.harris@thetrydaily.thm
Linkbit.ly/3sHkX3da12340

This one looked wrong before I even opened the SIEM. amazon.biz isn't Amazon, the bit.ly link hides where it actually goes, and the 48-hour deadline is textbook pressure. Pivoting on the link confirmed someone at TheTryDaily had already tried to click it — the firewall just got there first. No separate escalation needed here; that gets carried by the linked firewall event below.

The clearest one in the queue. m1crosoftsupport.co swaps a "1" for the "i" in Microsoft and isn't even a .com — real account security mail never comes from a domain like that. Pair the spoofed sender with a scare-location login alert and a link back to that same fake domain, and it's a straightforward credential-harvesting attempt. No internal correspondence backs this one up the way #8814 had — nothing legitimate ties to this domain at all.

Analyst Notes

What the queue taught me

Five alerts is a small sample, but the pattern held up across all of them.

01

Domain literacy beats domain familiarity

Check the actual string, not the display name. hrconnex.thm looked exactly as "external" as amazon.biz and m1crosoftsupport.co — only one of the three was real.

02

Urgency is a signal, not a verdict

Pressure language matters most when it's stacked with a spoofed sender or an obfuscated link — not when it's sitting on its own.

03

Pivot before you decide

The email alert alone never told the full story. Firewall logs and internal correspondence supplied the missing context every single time.

04

A block isn't a close

#8816 was already stopped at the firewall, but the blacklist doesn't cover unknown domains — so it still went up for escalation.

Result

Case closed

All three true positives got escalated and their infrastructure flagged. Both false positives — the same HR onboarding notice, sent twice — got documented and released without touching the user. The queue cleared, and the scenario marked it a win: 95 points, 1st place, and a true positive rate of 60% across the five alerts.

Not a flawless run — the recap flagged that mean-time-to-resolve and dwell time ran a little long overall — but the fastest single close, a phishing case, landed at 0.07 minutes. Room for speed, not for accuracy.
